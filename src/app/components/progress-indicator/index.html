The progress indicator component shows progress through steps toward a goal. The passive progress indicator represents steps that take place outside of the user’s control. The active progress indicator walks users through a multi-step process, either in the waterfall page (below) or in the Wizard component.

Use when

Use passive progress indicators when users must be aware of sequential progress that concerns them, but they are not responsible for.
Do use passive progress indicators to track sequential steps taken by other users in the system.

Don't use when

Don't use passive progress indicators when users must interact with the steps.
Don't use passive progress when users must take action on one or more steps.

Don’t use passive progress indicators to represent multiple independent statuses that are not sequentially related.
Don't use passive progress to track steps that have no sequence.

Don't use multiple passive progress indicators at once on items in a list.
Don't expose multiple passive progress indicators at once.
Do roll up steps that are inconsequential into a single status. Use a container like Popover or Flyout to hold the additional details the passive progress indicator provides.

Use when

Use waterfall progress indicators for workflows that require a long or complex multi-step setup process that will likely only be completed once. Waterfall progress indicators are ideal when these tasks may take more than one sitting, whether because of waiting for events outside of the system or because of the large amount of input required.

Use waterfall progress indicators when the number and name of steps is known, and the best experience requires at least three steps.
Do use waterfall progress indicators for complex configurations that are completed in stages.

Use waterfall progress indicators when the interaction complexity of the steps varies drastically. One step may involve complex interactions that are best performed in a full-screen modal, while another step may require a few simple inputs in a small modal. A single waterfall step can, itself, be a wizard modal if needed.
Do use modals of whatever size needed to contain the interactions in each waterfall step.
Don't mix modalities by putting inputs inside of a waterfall progress indicator

Don't use when

Don’t use waterfall progress indicators when a task is best presented as multiple setps but will be completed in a single sitting, or if the task is performed repeatedly. Use Wizards instead.
Don't use waterfall progress indicators for short interactions, even if they are best divided into separate steps.
Do use Wizards for multi-step tasks that are still short in duration.

Don’t use waterfall progress indicators when the required steps and their number is not known. If decisions will branch the workflow into different potential steps, arrange the workflow to make those decisions first, before the waterfall progress indicator is reached.

Don't present a waterfall progress indicator before the extent of the task is known.
Do present decisions that will determine the required steps before showing the steps themselves.

Don’t use waterfall progress indicators for editing existing properties once an initial configuration process has been completed. Edit related groups of properties inside of isolated modals.

If properties or settings are so interrelated or codependent that making changes to them requires reexamining and adjusting most of the other properties, it may be more appropriate to frame the task of editing them as “starting over” and begin the waterfall over again.
Don't revert a finished state back into a waterfall to edit it.
Do divide pieces of the finished state into groups that can edited in isolation.

Step details

You can provide addition details about a completed or current step. This can give extra context to the user, but avoid adding too many details or implying that the user should take action.

Waterfall title

It is usually best to include a title to reflect the context of the overall task. The title does not change as the user progresses through the waterfall steps.

Step details

You can provide additional details about a completed step, limited to a single field. Don’t go overboard; users can reopen the modal to review their choices if they need.

"Start over"

You can include an optional “start over” button that allows the user to erase all of the saved information for the waterfall process. This can return the user to the first step in the waterfall or, if some decision in the task occured before the waterfall, to another previous point in the workflow.

To guide users through the steps in a waterfall, only one action is “current” at a time. When the current interaction is completed (i.e. when its modal is saved), the waterfall advances to the next step by changing the styling of the action to the “previous” state, changing the step indicators to reflect the new current step, and displaying the new “current” action.

The previous step actions allow the user to review and/or edit earlier decisions, but the waterfall progress indicator does not “un-complete” any steps.

The final step modal in a waterfall progerss indicator is an opportunity to summarize the change to the system that will take effect and ask the user for confirmation. It is not necessary to review all of the information entered during the waterfall steps, but the final step must involve at least a simple confirmation. After the final step is completed, replace the waterfall progress indicator with the “finished” or “active” state of the system. This can be any page type (record page, work center, list page, etc.).

Use past tense verbs for step labels and keep them concise (e.g. Submitted, Approved, etc.). Include a datestamp in parentheses in most cases, but only include a timestamp if necessary. Use step details sparingly and keep them concise as well.

Step labels in waterfall progress indicators begin with the step number and describe the objective of the step concisely. Use a <verb> <direct object> format. The label of the last step in the waterfall should relate somehow to the transformation that takes place at the conclusion of the waterfall (“Activate”, “Tun on”, “Publish”, etc.).

The labels for “current step actions” should closely follow the description of the step label, but do not have to be identical. In particular, the action on the first step can be more generic and conversational (e.g. “Get started”). The labels for “previous step actions” should use verbs appropriate for post hoc actions (e.g. “Change”, “Edit”, etc.).

Active progress indicators

Active progress indicators illustrate the step-wise nature of tasks and reinforce progress as users complete steps. They isolate users inside the scope of tasks, contain all interactions necessary for each step, and provide controls to move through the steps.

Use active progress indicators when:

  • Tasks can be broken into discrete, predetermined steps.
  • The order of decisions within tasks matters because the system requires decisions in a certain order or because early decisions affect later decisions.

Do not use active progress indicators when:

  • The best experience requires fewer than three mandatory steps.
  • Decisions within tasks change the number of steps or the order of steps. Instead, move decisions that affect the number or order of steps to the start of workflows and branch users to different active progress contexts.
  • Users need to edit preset properties.
Modal wizards vs. waterfall pages

Modal wizards guide users through tasks that require a series of related decisions, while waterfall pages walk users through the steps to activate records or system settings that are created in an incomplete state. For more information about modal wizards, see the wizard component.

Use modal wizards when:

  • Tasks are best presented as multiple steps that users can complete in a single sitting.
  • Tasks do not result in records or settings with page-level views.
  • The interaction complexity of each step is low. For example, steps use standard form inputs and controls.
  • Tasks include no more than six steps.

Use waterfall pages when:

  • The complexity of tasks warrants a multi-step approach.
  • Tasks take a significant amount of time, and users may not complete the steps in a single sitting.
  • Tasks result in records or settings with page-level views.
  • Tasks complete initial setup or configuration for records or system settings that do not work until users complete steps that are infrequently revisited.
  • The complexity of steps varies drastically. For example, one step may involve interactions that are best performed in a full-screen modal while another step may require a small number of inputs or selections.
Passive progress indicators

Passive progress indicators inform users of progress that concerns them but that they are not responsible for. For example, passive progress indicators can track the progress of steps that other users in the system perform or steps that are completed outside the system. They provide details on an individually scoped level such as record pages, detail panels, or popovers.

Use passive progress indicators when:

  • Steps represent stages of completion that are essentially invisible to users because they take place outside of user control.
  • The stages of progression are known in advance and branching does not dynamically route processes.
  • Progress is represented in the individual context of record pages, detail panels, etc.

Do not use passive progress indicators when:

  • User interaction is required.
  • Steps represent multiple independent statuses that are not sequentially related.
  • Progress is represented in collective context such as lists, repeaters, and tables.
  • Statuses drive decision-making or user responses.
  • Combined with other passive indicators to create excessive noise for information that does not drive user action.
